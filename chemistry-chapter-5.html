Introduction to Electrochemistry

Electrochemistry

Branch of chemistry that deals with the relationship between electrical energy and chemical reactions, studying processes where chemical energy is converted to electrical energy and vice versa.

Key Concepts:

  • Electrolytes: Substances that conduct electricity when dissolved in water or molten state
  • Electrodes: Conductors through which electric current enters or leaves an electrolyte
  • Electrochemical cells: Devices that convert chemical energy to electrical energy (voltaic cells) or use electrical energy to drive chemical reactions (electrolytic cells)
  • Redox reactions: Reactions involving transfer of electrons (reduction and oxidation)
Types of Electrochemical Cells
  1. Voltaic/Galvanic Cells: Convert chemical energy to electrical energy (spontaneous redox reactions)
  2. Electrolytic Cells: Use electrical energy to drive non-spontaneous redox reactions
  3. Concentration Cells: Type of voltaic cell with same electrodes but different electrolyte concentrations

Electrode Potential

Electrode Potential (E)

The potential difference developed between an electrode and its electrolyte when the electrode is in contact with its ions at unit activity (1M concentration at 25¬∞C).

Standard Electrode Potential (E¬∞)

The electrode potential measured under standard conditions:

  • Temperature: 25¬∞C (298 K)
  • Pressure: 1 atm (for gases)
  • Concentration: 1 M

Electrochemical Series

Arrangement of elements in order of their standard reduction potentials.

Important Points
  • Elements with negative E¬∞ values: Good reducing agents
  • Elements with positive E¬∞ values: Good oxidizing agents
  • More negative E¬∞ ‚Üí Stronger reducing agent
  • More positive E¬∞ ‚Üí Stronger oxidizing agent
  • Li has most negative E¬∞ (-3.05 V) ‚Üí Strongest reducing agent
  • F‚ÇÇ has most positive E¬∞ (+2.87 V) ‚Üí Strongest oxidizing agent

Applications of Electrochemical Series:

  • Predicting feasibility of redox reactions: Reaction is spontaneous if E¬∞cell > 0
  • Displacement reactions: More reactive metal displaces less reactive metal from its salt solution
  • Relative oxidizing/reducing power: Determining strength of oxidizing/reducing agents
  • Protection from corrosion: Sacrificial anode protection

Electrochemical Cells

Voltaic Cell (Galvanic Cell)

Device that converts chemical energy to electrical energy through spontaneous redox reactions.

Components:

  • Two different electrodes (anode and cathode)
  • Electrolyte solutions
  • Salt bridge or porous partition
  • External circuit with voltmeter

Cell Potential (Ecell)

The potential difference between two electrodes of an electrochemical cell.

E¬∞cell = E¬∞cathode - E¬∞anode = E¬∞right - E¬∞left

For spontaneous reaction: E¬∞cell > 0

Salt Bridge

U-shaped tube containing concentrated solution of inert electrolyte (KCl, KNO‚ÇÉ, NH‚ÇÑNO‚ÇÉ).

Functions:

  • Completes electrical circuit
  • Maintains electrical neutrality
  • Prevents mixing of solutions
  • Minimizes liquid junction potential
Cell Representation Rules
  • Anode written on left, Cathode on right
  • Single vertical line (|) represents phase boundary
  • Double vertical line (||) represents salt bridge
  • Concentrations are specified if not 1M
  • Gas electrodes: Pt | gas | ion

Multiple Choice Questions

  • A) Fluorine
