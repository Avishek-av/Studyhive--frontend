Thermodynamics

Study of energy transformations in chemical reactions, including concepts of internal energy, enthalpy, entropy, spontaneity, and Gibbs free energy. Covers the laws of thermodynamics and their applications.

Introduction to Thermodynamics

Thermodynamics

Branch of physical chemistry that deals with the relationships between heat, work, temperature, and energy in chemical reactions and physical transformations.

Scope: Thermodynamics studies energy changes during chemical reactions, predicting spontaneity, and determining equilibrium conditions.

System and Surroundings
  • System: The portion of the universe under study
  • Surroundings: Everything else in the universe
  • Boundary: The surface separating system from surroundings

Types of Systems:

  • Open System: Can exchange both matter and energy with surroundings
  • Closed System: Can exchange only energy, not matter
  • Isolated System: Cannot exchange either matter or energy
Internal Energy (U)

The total energy contained within a system, including kinetic and potential energy of molecules.

ŒîU = q + w

Where: ŒîU = change in internal energy, q = heat absorbed, w = work done on system

State Functions

Properties that depend only on the current state of the system, not on how it reached that state. Examples: Internal energy (U), Enthalpy (H), Entropy (S), Gibbs free energy (G).

Laws of Thermodynamics

First Law of Thermodynamics

"Energy can neither be created nor destroyed, only converted from one form to another."

ŒîU = q + w

Or: q = ŒîU - w

Sign Convention:

  • Heat absorbed by system: q = +ve
  • Heat released by system: q = -ve
  • Work done on system: w = +ve
  • Work done by system: w = -ve
Second Law of Thermodynamics

"In any spontaneous process, the total entropy of the universe increases."

ŒîSuniverse = ŒîSsystem + ŒîSsurroundings > 0
Third Law of Thermodynamics

"The entropy of a perfect crystalline substance approaches zero as the temperature approaches absolute zero (0 K)."

Solution: q = +100 J, w = -40 J

ŒîU = q + w = 100 + (-40) = 60 J

Factors Affecting Entropy
  • Temperature: Entropy increases with temperature
  • Physical State: S(solid) < S(liquid) < S(gas)
  • Molecular Complexity: More complex molecules have higher entropy
  • Number of Particles: More particles = more entropy

Predicting Entropy Changes:

  • ŒîS > 0: Increase in disorder (favorable)
  • ŒîS < 0: Decrease in disorder (unfavorable)
  • ŒîS = 0: No change in disorder

ŒîS < 0: Water freezing (S decreases), Gas compression

Multiple Choice Questions

1. Which law of thermodynamics states that energy cannot be created or destroyed?
  • A) Zeroth law
  • B) First law
  • C) Second law
  • D) Third law
Answer: B) First law of thermodynamics
2. For a spontaneous process at constant T and P:
  • A) ŒîG > 0
  • B) ŒîG = 0
  • C) ŒîG < 0
  • D) ŒîG = ŒîH
Answer: C) ŒîG < 0 (Gibbs free energy decreases for spontaneous processes)
3. The mathematical expression of first law of thermodynamics is:
  • A) ŒîU = q - w
  • B) ŒîU = q + w
  • C) ŒîU = w - q
  • D) ŒîU = q/w
Answer: B) ŒîU = q + w (Change in internal energy equals heat added plus work done on system)
4. Which of the following is a state function?
  • A) Work
  • B) Heat
  • C) Enthalpy
  • D) Both work and heat
Answer: C) Enthalpy (State functions: U, H, S, G. Path functions: q, w)
