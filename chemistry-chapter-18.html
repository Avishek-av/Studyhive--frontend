Polymers

Definition: Polymers are macromolecules formed by the repetition of small units called monomers through chemical bonds.

Polymerization Reaction

Classification of Polymers:

Based on Source
Natural: Cotton, silk, wool, natural rubber
Synthetic: Nylon, polyester, PVC, Teflon
Semi-synthetic: Rayon, cellulose acetate
Based on Structure
Linear: Polyethylene, PVC
Branched: Low density polyethylene
Cross-linked: Bakelite, vulcanized rubber
Based on Molecular Forces
Elastomers: Rubber, buna-S
Fibers: Nylon, polyester, silk
Thermoplastics: Polythene, PVC
Thermosetting: Bakelite, melamine

Dyes

Definition: Dyes are colored organic compounds that can impart color to substrates (textiles, paper, leather, food) to which they are applied.

Characteristics of Good Dyes
  • Should have suitable color
  • Should be able to fix itself to fabric
  • Should be fast to light and washing
  • Should be resistant to action of acids, alkalies, and oxidizing agents
  • Should be non-toxic and eco-friendly

Classification Based on Application Method:

Direct Dyes
Applied directly to fabric from aqueous solution. Example: Congo red
Vat Dyes
Applied in reduced soluble form, oxidized to insoluble form. Example: Indigo
Mordant Dyes
Require mordant (metal salt) to fix dye. Example: Alizarin
Disperse Dyes
Applied from fine aqueous suspension. Used for synthetic fibers.
Reactive Dyes
Form covalent bond with fiber. Example: Procion dyes
Acid Dyes
Applied from acidic bath. Used for wool, silk. Example: Methyl orange

Classification Based on Chemical Structure:

1. Azo Dyes: Contain -N=N- group. Largest class of synthetic dyes.

Example: Methyl Orange (pH indicator)

2. Anthraquinone Dyes: Derived from anthraquinone. Example: Alizarin.

3. Triphenylmethane Dyes: Contain triphenylmethane skeleton. Example: Malachite green.

4. Indigoid Dyes: Contain indigotin structure. Example: Indigo (oldest known dye).

5. Phthalein Dyes: Used as indicators. Example: Phenolphthalein.

Important Synthetic Dyes:

Mauveine
First synthetic dye discovered by W.H. Perkin in 1856
Alizarin
Originally from madder root, now synthesized from anthraquinone
Indigo
Natural blue dye from Indigofera plant, now synthesized

Color and Chemical Structure Relationship:

  • Chromophore: Groups responsible for color (C=O, N=N, NO‚ÇÇ, C=S)
  • Auxochrome: Groups that intensify color (OH, NH‚ÇÇ, COOH, SO‚ÇÉH)
  • Bathochromic shift: Shift to longer wavelength (red shift)
  • Hypsochromic shift: Shift to shorter wavelength (blue shift)

Drugs and Pharmaceuticals

Definition: Drugs are chemical substances used in the diagnosis, prevention, and treatment of diseases.

Characteristics of Ideal Drugs
  • Should not be toxic or have minimal side effects
  • Should be effective in low concentrations
  • Should not accumulate in the body
  • Should be stable and have good shelf life
  • Should be compatible with other drugs

Classification of Drugs:

1. Based on Origin:

Natural Drugs
Derived from plants, animals, minerals
Examples: Quinine, morphine, digitalis
Synthetic Drugs
Prepared by chemical synthesis
Examples: Aspirin, paracetamol, sulpha drugs
Semi-synthetic Drugs
Natural drugs modified chemically
Examples: Heroin (from morphine), ampicillin

2. Based on Therapeutic Action:

Analgesics
Relieve pain. Examples: Aspirin, paracetamol, morphine
Antipyretics
Reduce fever. Examples: Paracetamol, aspirin
Antibiotics
Kill/inhibit microorganisms. Examples: Penicillin, tetracycline
Antiseptics
Kill microorganisms on living tissues. Example: Dettol
Disinfectants
Kill microorganisms on non-living objects. Example: Phenol
Tranquilizers
Reduce anxiety and tension. Examples: Equanil, valium
Antacids
Neutralize stomach acid. Examples: Milk of magnesia, ranitidine
Antihistamines
Counteract histamine in allergies. Examples: Avil, benadryl

Important Drugs:

Aspirin (Acetylsalicylic acid):

Synthesis: Salicylic acid + Acetic anhydride ‚Üí Aspirin + Acetic acid
  • Analgesic, antipyretic, anti-inflammatory
  • Also used as blood thinner (anti-platelet)
  • Side effect: Gastric irritation

Paracetamol (Acetaminophen):

  • Analgesic and antipyretic
  • Less gastric irritation than aspirin
  • Overdose causes liver damage

Penicillin: First antibiotic discovered by Alexander Fleming (1928).

Stimulants: Caffeine, cocaine - Increase activity

Chemotherapy: Treatment of diseases using chemical substances. Paul Ehrlich is called "father of chemotherapy."

Pesticides

Definition: Pesticides are chemical substances used to kill pests (insects, weeds, fungi, rodents) that damage crops, transmit diseases, or cause nuisance.

Classification of Pesticides
Insecticides
Kill insects. Examples: DDT, BHC, Malathion
Herbicides
Kill weeds. Examples: 2,4-D, Atrazine
Fungicides
Kill fungi. Examples: Bordeaux mixture, Dithane
Rodenticides
Kill rodents. Example: Warfarin

Insecticides:

1. Organochlorines:

  • DDT (Dichloro-diphenyl-trichloroethane): First synthetic insecticide, now banned in many countries due to persistence in environment
  • BHC (Benzene hexachloride): Also called Lindane, used against agricultural pests
  • Aldrin and Dieldrin: Highly toxic, persistent in environment

2. Organophosphates: More biodegradable than organochlorines.

  • Malathion: Low toxicity to mammals, used in public health
  • Parathion: Highly toxic, requires careful handling
  • Chlorpyrifos: Broad-spectrum insecticide

3. Carbamates: Derived from carbamic acid.

  • Carbaryl (Sevin): Broad-spectrum insecticide
  • Aldicarb: Systemic insecticide, highly toxic

4. Pyrethroids: Synthetic analogs of natural pyrethrins.

  • Permethrin: Low toxicity to mammals
  • Cypermethrin: Used in agriculture and public health

Herbicides:

2,4-D (2,4-Dichlorophenoxyacetic acid)
Selective herbicide, kills broad-leaved weeds, mimics plant hormone auxin
Atrazine
Selective herbicide for maize fields, inhibits photosynthesis
Paraquat
Non-selective herbicide, highly toxic to humans

Fungicides:

  • Bordeaux mixture: Copper sulfate + calcium hydroxide, first fungicide
  • Dithane (Mancozeb): Broad-spectrum fungicide
  • Sulfur: Oldest fungicide, used as dust or spray

Problems with Pesticides:

‚ö†Ô∏è Environmental and Health Issues

Bioaccumulation: Pesticides accumulate in food chain

Biomagnification: Concentration increases at higher trophic levels

Persistence: Some pesticides don't degrade easily (DDT, BHC)

Resistance: Pests develop resistance to pesticides

Non-target effects: Harm beneficial insects, birds, and animals

Integrated Pest Management (IPM): Alternative approach using combination of methods:

  • Biological control (using natural predators)
  • Cultural practices (crop rotation, intercropping)
  • Mechanical methods (traps, barriers)
  • Chemical pesticides as last resort

Modern Applications and Future

Green Chemistry in Polymer Industry:

Biodegradable Polymers
PLA (polylactic acid), PHA (polyhydroxyalkanoates) - Reduce plastic pollution
Recycled Polymers
PET recycling, plastic waste management
Smart Polymers
Respond to stimuli (temperature, pH) - drug delivery systems

Advancements in Dye Technology:

  • Natural dyes revival: Eco-friendly alternatives to synthetic dyes
  • Digital textile printing: Reduced water consumption
  • Nanotechnology in dyes: Enhanced color fastness, UV protection
  • Bio-based dyes: From microorganisms and agricultural waste

Pharmaceutical Innovations:

Targeted Drug Delivery
Nanoparticles deliver drugs to specific cells, reduce side effects
Biopharmaceuticals
Therapeutic proteins, monoclonal antibodies, vaccines
Personalized Medicine
Drugs tailored to individual genetic makeup
3D Printed Drugs
Customized dosage and drug combinations

Sustainable Pesticides:

  • Biopesticides: From natural materials (plants, bacteria, minerals)
  • Pheromone traps: Disrupt mating of pest insects
  • RNA interference: Gene silencing technology for pest control
  • Precision agriculture: Drones and sensors for targeted pesticide application

Chemistry in Everyday Life:

Food Chemistry
Preservatives, antioxidants, flavors, nutritional supplements
Cosmetic Chemistry
Skincare products, sunscreens, hair dyes, perfumes
Materials Science
Smart materials, superconductors, nanomaterials
Environmental Chemistry
Water treatment, air purification, waste management
Chemistry for Sustainable Development

Green Chemistry Principles: Prevent waste, use renewable feedstocks, design safer chemicals

Circular Economy: Design products for reuse, recycling, and recovery

UN Sustainable Development Goals: Chemistry contributes to clean water, affordable energy, good health

Multiple Choice Questions

Which polymer is used for non-stick cookware?
  • A) Polyethylene
  • B) PVC
  • C) Teflon
  • D) Nylon
Answer: C) Teflon (Polytetrafluoroethylene)
Which is a natural polymer?
  • A) Nylon
  • B) Polyester
  • C) Silk
  • D) PVC
Answer: C) Silk (Protein polymer from silkworms)
The first synthetic dye discovered was:
  • A) Indigo
  • B) Alizarin
  • C) Mauveine
  • D) Congo red
Answer: C) Mauveine (Discovered by W.H. Perkin in 1856)
Aspirin is chemically known as:
  • A) Salicylic acid
  • B) Acetylsalicylic acid
  • C) Methyl salicylate
  • D) Para-aminophenol
Answer: B) Acetylsalicylic acid
DDT is an example of:
  • A) Herbicide
  • B) Fungicide
  • C) Insecticide
  • D) Rodenticide
Answer: C) Insecticide (Organochlorine insecticide)
Which is NOT a characteristic of ideal drug?
  • A) Non-toxic
  • B) Effective in low dose
  • C) Accumulates in body
  • D) Stable shelf life
Answer: C) Accumulates in body (Drugs should not accumulate, should be excreted)
