Introduction to Chemical Kinetics

Chemical Kinetics

The branch of physical chemistry that studies the rates of chemical reactions, the factors affecting these rates, and the mechanisms by which reactions occur.

Order and Molecularity

Order of Reaction

The sum of powers to which the concentration terms are raised in the rate law equation.

Characteristics:

  • Can be zero, fractional, or integer
  • Determined experimentally
  • May not be equal to stoichiometric coefficients
  • Can change with conditions
Molecularity

The number of molecules, atoms, or ions that must collide simultaneously to result in a chemical reaction.

Types:

  • Unimolecular: One molecule (decomposition: N‚ÇÇO‚ÇÖ ‚Üí 2NO‚ÇÇ + ¬ΩO‚ÇÇ)

Collision Theory

Collision Theory

Chemical reactions occur when reactant molecules collide with sufficient energy and proper orientation.

Activation Energy (E‚Çê)

The minimum energy that colliding molecules must possess for a reaction to occur.

Activated Complex (Transition State)

An unstable intermediate formed during the collision of reactant molecules. It has higher energy than both reactants and products.

Energy Profile Diagram

Energy of reactants < Energy of activated complex

Effective Collisions

For reaction to occur, collisions must be:

Rate of reaction depends on:

Catalysis

Catalyst

A substance that alters the rate of a chemical reaction without itself undergoing any permanent chemical change.

Types of Catalysis:

3. Enzyme Catalysis

Biological catalysts (proteins) that are highly specific and efficient.

Characteristics:

  • High specificity (lock and key model)
  • Optimum temperature and pH
  • Can be inhibited by certain substances
  • Follow Michaelis-Menten kinetics
Characteristics of Catalysts
  • Remains unchanged chemically at the end of reaction
  • Required in small amounts
  • Does not affect equilibrium position
  • Speeds up both forward and reverse reactions
  • Specific in action
